The Passage

Ephesians 6:10–20

10Finally, be strong in the Lord and in his mighty power. 11Put on the full armor of God, so that you can take your stand against the devil's schemes. 12For our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the powers of this dark world and against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly realms.

13Therefore put on the full armor of God, so that when the day of evil comes, you may be able to stand your ground, and after you have done everything, to stand. 14Stand firm then, with the belt of truth buckled around your waist, with the breastplate of righteousness in place, 15and with your feet fitted with the readiness that comes from the gospel of peace.

16In addition to all this, take up the shield of faith, with which you can extinguish all the flaming arrows of the evil one. 17Take the helmet of salvation and the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God.

18And pray in the Spirit on all occasions with all kinds of prayers and requests. With this in mind, be alert and always keep on praying for all the Lord's people. 19Pray also for me, that whenever I speak, words may be given me so that I will fearlessly make known the mystery of the gospel, 20for which I am an ambassador in chains. Pray that I may declare it fearlessly, as I should.

Context & Background

Historical & Theological Depth

Paul wrote Ephesians around AD 60–62 during his Roman imprisonment (Ephesians 3:1, 4:1, 6:20). The letter was likely a circular letter meant for the church in Ephesus and other congregations in Asia Minor.

The first three chapters focus on who we are in Christ — chosen, adopted, redeemed, seated with Him in the heavenly realms. The last three chapters teach us how to live out that identity. The armor of God passage is the climactic call to action: because of who you are in Christ (chapters 1–3) and how you are called to live (chapters 4–6), stand firm against the enemy.

Paul was likely chained to a Roman soldier as he dictated this letter (Acts 28:16, 20). The imagery would have been vivid to both writer and reader. The Roman legionary was the most formidable soldier of the ancient world, and every piece of his equipment had a specific defensive or offensive purpose.

But Paul also draws on the Old Testament. Much of this imagery comes directly from Isaiah, where God Himself is described as wearing armor:

Isaiah 59:17 "He put on righteousness as his breastplate, and the helmet of salvation on his head; he put on the garments of vengeance and wrapped himself in zeal as in a cloak."
Isaiah 11:5 "Righteousness will be his belt and faithfulness the sash around his waist."

The armor we are called to wear is God's own armor. We stand in His strength, not ours.

The Greek word for "stand" (stēnai) appears four times in this passage (vv. 11, 13, 14). It is a defensive military term — to hold one's ground, to not retreat. Paul is not calling believers to charge into enemy territory on their own initiative. He is calling us to hold the position Christ has already won.

The victory is already accomplished (Colossians 2:15). Our job is to stand in it. This is a posture of confident dependence, not anxious effort.

Verse 12 is crucial: "For our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the powers of this dark world and against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly realms."

This reframes every conflict a believer faces. The coworker who wrongs you, the family member who hurts you, the neighbor who opposes you — these are not the enemy. There are spiritual realities behind human opposition, and the armor of God is designed to help us fight those unseen battles through prayer, truth, and faith, not by attacking people.

2 Corinthians 10:3–4 "For though we live in the world, we do not wage war as the world does. The weapons we fight with are not the weapons of the world. On the contrary, they have divine power to demolish strongholds."

Prayer is not a seventh piece of armor — it is the atmosphere in which all the armor functions. Notice Paul's language: "pray in the Spirit on all occasions with all kinds of prayers and requests." The repetition of "all" (four times in verse 18 in the Greek) is striking.

Paul also asks prayer for himself — that he would speak fearlessly. Even the apostle, wearing the full armor of God, needed the prayers of the saints. Spiritual warfare is a team activity.

Five of the six pieces are defensive: belt, breastplate, shoes, shield, helmet. Only the sword is offensive. The emphasis is clearly on standing firm rather than attacking.

But even the "sword of the Spirit" — the rhema (spoken word) of God — was Jesus' own weapon against Satan in the wilderness. Three times He responded "It is written…" (Matthew 4). The Word of God, spoken in faith, pushes back the enemy. This is why Scripture memorization and meditation matter so deeply.

Questions for Reflection

I.
Which piece of the armor do you most often forget to "put on" in your daily life? What would it look like this week to wear it intentionally?
II.
When you face conflict, do you instinctively see "flesh and blood" as the enemy, or do you discern the spiritual battle behind it? How might verse 12 change how you respond to difficult people?
III.
Paul uses the word "stand" four times. In what area of your life is God calling you to simply stand firm rather than retreat or run?
IV.
The sword of the Spirit is the Word of God. Do you have Scripture ready to speak when temptation comes? What is one verse you could commit to memory this week?
V.
Paul, though an apostle in chains, asked for prayer for boldness. Who in your life needs you to intercede for them today — and who can you ask to pray for you?
